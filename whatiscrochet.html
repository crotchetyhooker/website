What is Crochet?

Learn here just what this mysterious craft is and why you should be addicted to it.

Crochet is Not Knitting

Crochet is a type of handicraft where you use a hook and some yarn to make basically anything. The word 'crochet' is actually just the French word for 'hook' because for some ungodly reason we allowed the French to name things.

Crochet is different from knitting in two crucial ways:

  • Crochet uses just a hook. Knitting requires two, non-hooked needles. Sometimes more.
  • Crochet stitches don't have 'live' stitches. In knitting, stitches are attached to one needle (the live ones) and are then worked onto another needle.

​​

Should I knit or crochet?

When you say 'should' what becomes important are your concerns. Are you asking which is cheaper? Which is easier? Or which one will make what a certain object? Let's compare:

Price:
Crochet is cheaper. Not because of the yarn used (any yarn you can knit with, you can crochet with and vice versa) but because knitting requires more tools. Knitting requires many types of needles but you really only need one set of hooks.

Easier:
Lots of people say crochet is easier and lots of people say knitting is easier. It's different for everyone. The truth is crochet is better.

Any other differences?

Strictly speaking, anything that can be knitted can be crocheted and vice versa. Typically, knitting will yield thinner and stretchier work whereas crochet is a little thicker and stronger. Crochet tends to be much more popular for making blankets, household items and dolls. But, as mentioned before, the sky is the limit.

Learning Resources

You can learn from my Crochet Academy or various websites, books and even teaching videos.

But the best way to learn is from a teacher. Find your local yarn shop and sign up for classes. I teach at one myself and I can attest that when the internet fails is usually when people turn to people. However, it's best not to wait until that point. In the interest of not developing bad habits, start with a real teacher.
